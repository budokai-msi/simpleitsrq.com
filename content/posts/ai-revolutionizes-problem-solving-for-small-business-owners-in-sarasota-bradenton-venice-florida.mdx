## Short answer

AI is genuinely good at chipping away at the repetitive work that clogs a small office. But the first useful AI project is almost never the exciting one. It is small, boring, and measurable: one repeatable workflow, with clean inputs and a human who checks the output before it counts.

"Make the company more AI-powered" is not a project. "Turn voicemails into draft service tickets" is.

## Pick one workflow that wastes time today

Start with a process that has a clear before-and-after, so you can actually tell whether the tool helped:

- Turning voicemail notes into draft service tickets
- Summarizing intake forms before a call
- Drafting a first response to common customer questions
- Sorting vendor invoices for human review
- Pulling action items out of a meeting recording

The common thread: each one is repetitive, low-stakes, and easy to check. If a mistake slips through, someone catches it in seconds - not in a lawsuit.

## The three checks before real data touches a tool

Before any AI tool touches customer data, confirm three things:

- **Data rules.** Who is allowed to paste what into the tool? Is it a company account or someone's personal login?
- **Identity and ownership.** Which account owns the output and the history? Can you turn off access when someone leaves?
- **The review step.** Who reads the output before a customer or a record sees it?

If you cannot answer those three for a workflow, it is not ready for AI yet - it is ready for a five-minute policy conversation first. Our [one-page AI work policy](/blog/sarasota-employees-using-ai-work-policy) is built exactly for that.

## Measure it like any other change

The reason "small and boring" beats "transformative" is that you can measure small and boring. Before you start, write down what the task costs today - minutes per ticket, hours per week, error rate. After two weeks with the tool, measure again. If it did not move, change the workflow or drop it. That discipline is what separates a real productivity gain from an expensive subscription nobody uses.

## What to do this week

1. Name the single most repetitive task in your office.
2. Write down what it costs today in time or errors.
3. Confirm the three checks above for that task's data.
4. Run a two-week pilot on that one workflow only.
5. Compare the numbers and decide: keep, fix, or drop.
