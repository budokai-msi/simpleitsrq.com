## Short answer

When old software makes the news, the fun part is the nostalgia. The useful part for a small office is the reminder: **your systems will outlive the people who set them up.** The business has to know what it owns, how it is backed up, and who can still support it - before the one person who remembers walks out the door.

The fix is not exciting. It is a written record of how your office actually runs. Most Sarasota and Bradenton offices we walk into do not have one, and every emergency we get called into is harder because of it.

## The real risk: invisible systems

Legacy systems are not dangerous because they are old. They are dangerous when they are *invisible* - load-bearing but undocumented:

- One workstation runs a program nobody else understands, and it never gets patched because nobody dares touch it.
- A vendor portal depends on an old browser or a Java plugin that the next Windows update will break.
- Backups exist but have never been restored, so nobody actually knows if they work.
- License keys live in an email thread from four years ago, in an inbox that has since been deleted.
- Nobody knows which device talks to the copier, the camera recorder, or the line-of-business app.

That is how a tiny outage becomes a multi-day scramble - not because the problem was hard, but because no one knew where to start.

## Document before you replace

Before ripping out anything old, write down five things about it:

1. **The workflow.** What does this system actually do, and who depends on it?
2. **The files.** Where does its data live, and what format is it in?
3. **The credentials.** Who has the logins and license keys, and where are they stored?
4. **The dependencies.** What OS, browser, plugin, or other device does it need to keep working?
5. **The recovery path.** If it died today, what is the exact sequence to bring it back?

Only after that do you decide whether to keep, isolate, replace, or migrate it. Replacing a system you do not understand is how data gets lost.

## A weekend documentation pass any office can do

You do not need a consultant for the first version:

- Walk the office and photograph every server, switch, NAS, and "do not turn off" machine, including the model stickers.
- Open the network closet and **label every cable and port** at both ends. A cheap label maker pays for itself the first time you trace a dead jack: <Affiliate token="amazon_search:label maker for office cables|a handheld label maker" />.
- Put credentials and license keys into a real password manager, not a spreadsheet.
- Write the five points above for each load-bearing system into one shared document.

That document is the difference between a 20-minute fix and a lost afternoon.

## What to do this week

1. Name the one machine or app nobody is allowed to touch. That is your highest risk.
2. Confirm its backup has actually been restored at least once.
3. Find out who still knows how to support it - and what happens when they leave.
4. Write down its five facts above.
5. Decide: keep, isolate, replace, or migrate.
